### Chapter 25: The Dagshelgr Invocation
Eragon wakes early to find the elves Narí and Lifaen already awake and watching for dangers in the forest. During a conversation with Arya, Eragon feels his hammer amulet grow hot as someone attempts to scry him, though Saphira helps him resist the drain on his energy. The group continues their journey up the Gaena River until they reach a massive waterfall that requires a difficult portage. Lifaen objects to using Saphira as a beast of burden, but she ignores him and flies the supplies upstream of her own free will. Arya laughs for the first time, then instructs Eragon to hide his human ears and Saphira to travel only at night to avoid being seen before they meet the queen.

Saphira reluctantly agrees to separate from Eragon during the day, fearing for his safety while his back is still crippled. Eragon wears his hooded cape as they bypass the elven city of Sílthrim, which glows with mysterious silver lights in the distance. That night, a haunting and powerful melody drifts from the city, nearly enchanting Eragon and the dwarf Orik. Arya explains that it is Dagshelgr, a spring ritual where elves sing in the ancient language to ensure the forest remains fertile and healthy. The music causes a frenzy among the local animals and visibly affects Saphira, who feels a deep connection to the life force of the woods.

By dawn, the forest shows visible signs of new growth and the air smells fresh and clean. Arya sends Narí and Lifaen to Sílthrim to fetch horses and request reinforcements for the border post at Ceris. The elves return with six magnificent white stallions that respond to commands in the ancient language and require no saddles or harnesses. Eragon is assigned a horse named Folkvír, while Saphira expresses a sudden, deep sadness over being the last of her kind without a mate. Eragon comforts her by promising to help her find other dragons once they are free of their current obligations.



### Chapter 26: The Pinewood City
Eragon struggles with the dense and repetitive scenery of Du Weldenvarden, feeling hopelessly lost without the guidance of the elves. The trees grow progressively larger as they approach the elven capital, with trunks reaching over two hundred feet in height. Arya warns Eragon that they have triggered protective wards and must remain on the path as they enter her world. They encounter Gilderien the Wise, a silver-circleted guardian who permits them to pass after inspecting Eragon's ring and palm. The forest eventually thins to reveal a glade filled with flowers and buildings that grow directly out of the living pine trees.

The city of Ellesméra features graceful structures like hexagonal tiered houses and multi-level dwellings reinforced by flying buttresses. Eragon observes the elven inhabitants, noting their ethereal beauty and rustic clothing trimmed with autumnal colors. The elves greet the party with songs and praise Saphira with titles such as "Daughter of Air and Fire." Arya explains that these buildings and tools are created by singing to the forest in the ancient language to shape its growth. The group follows a tourmaline-studded path toward a grand hall made from the boles of living trees.

Inside the hall, twenty-four elven lords and ladies sit in wait, observing the newcomers with wonder and hope. At the head of the assembly, Queen Islanzadí sits upon a throne of knotted roots under a white pavilion. She wears a crimson tunic and a velvet cloak, appearing both proud and fragile as she watches them approach. A white raven named Blagden perches near her and croaks a single, prophetic word as they enter. Arya kneels before the queen, who descends from her throne and tearfully embraces her daughter, admitting she had wronged her.



### Chapter 27: QUEEN ISLANZADÍ

Eragon is shocked to discover that Arya is a princess, which he feels creates a new barrier between them. Queen Islanzadí and Arya share a tense reconciliation, acknowledging the seventy years they spent apart after Arya was banned from the queen's presence. Eragon uses the formal elven greeting he learned, surprising the council with his knowledge of their customs. He introduces himself as Eragon Shadeslayer, a title that causes a visible stir among the assembled elven nobility. The queen requests the full story of their journey, Brom's death, and the mission that brought them to the forest.

After hearing the narrative and reading Nasuada's scroll, Islanzadí regrets her previous decision to withdraw support from the Varden. She pledges to assist the human rebels once again and names Eragon "Elf Friend," allowing him to keep Brom's ring, Aren. The queen expresses concern over Eragon's back injury, stating that his training may be futile unless he can overcome the Shade's magic. Arya recounts her imprisonment and torture in Gil'ead, which moves the elven lords to cold anger and the queen to tears. To mark the occasion, the queen magically showers the hall with roses and lilies before announcing a great feast.

The celebration features music from harps and pipes, along with a potent elven liqueur called faelnirv that provides clarity and energy. Eragon meets a werecat named Maud, who recognizes his connection to Solembum and warns him not to be a laggard. At the end of the meal, Islanzadí leads Eragon and Saphira to a series of globular rooms high in a tree, which once served as the home of the leader of the Riders. Eragon explores the simple but elegant living quarters, which include a study and a padded roost for Saphira. He eventually falls asleep snuggled against Saphira's side, overwhelmed by his new surroundings in the heart of the elven capital.



### Chapter 28: Out of the Past
Eragon wakes at dawn and finds two trays of fruit and a bundle of new clothes left for him by the elven servants. A note from Bellaen of House Miolandra explains that elves do not hunt or eat meat, so Saphira must hunt for herself in the forest. The new garments include russet tunics and soft leggings that are of much higher quality than his travel-worn clothes. Orik arrives and warns Eragon that the queen is tense and that something important is about to happen. They descend the tree to find Islanzadí waiting for them in a mantle of white swan feathers.

The queen leads them to a remote area of Ellesméra where she demands they swear an oath of secrecy in the ancient language. She explains that this is necessary to protect their greatest advantage over Galbatorix and ensure their secrets are never revealed. Orik reluctantly agrees after being assured that the oath is for the safety of the Varden and the dwarves. Eragon and Saphira also deliver the required oath, bound by the magic of the elven tongue. The group then ascends a wooded knoll that ends at a high stone cliff overlooking the vast forest.

As they stand at the edge of the world, Eragon recognizes the location from a previous vision he had of a mysterious figure. The air suddenly shivers with a series of heavy, rhythmic thuds that make Eragon's teeth chatter with the pressure. A sudden gust of wind bends the clover as the source of the noise approaches from below the cliff. The elves stand perfectly still while Eragon and his companions watch the horizon in anticipation. Finally, a massive gold dragon with a Rider on its back rises from beneath the cliff's edge to meet them.



### Chapter 29: Conviction
In Carvahall, Roran recovers from his injuries while Horst explains that the villagers could not continue the fight after Roran fainted. Roran is devastated by the loss of Katrina and feels torn between his love for her and his loyalty to his home. He realizes that staying in the valley will not help him rescue her, but he cannot abandon the village to the Empire's soldiers. After a period of intense grief and despair, he uses his will to master his emotions and seek a rational solution. He decides that the only way to save both his people and Katrina is to lead the entire village to the Varden.

Roran plans a daring escape across the Spine to the port of Narda, where they can take a ship south to Surda. He understands that he must convince the farmers and merchants to abandon their property and lives to avoid slavery or death. To gain their support, he intends to inspire them with a sense of purpose and destiny rather than just fear. He rises from his bed despite his pain and gathers the villagers in the center of town using a torch and a powerful summons. He shows them his bleeding hand as a symbol of the pain they will all suffer if they do not resist the Empire.

During his speech, Roran lists the names of the fallen and decries the tyranny of King Galbatorix and his demonic servants. He declares his intention to march in two days and invites anyone who wishes to live free to join him. Several villagers, including the widow Birgit and the farmer Delwin, immediately step forward to pledge their support for the journey. Horst concludes the meeting by telling everyone to take the night to reflect on the monumental decision before them. Roran returns to his room, hearing the villagers begin to discuss the fate of their families and the future of Carvahall.
