### Chapter 6
Feyre arrives at a sprawling, opulent estate covered in roses and ivy that exists in a state of eternal spring. She considers escaping south toward the wall but realizes she is too weak from hunger to survive the journey. Inside the manor, she finds a lavish dining room filled with familiar human foods and encounter a beast that transforms into a golden-haired High Fae man. This man, who wears an emerald-embedded golden mask, identifies himself as one of the ruling nobility and insists that Feyre eat. Feyre initially refuses the food, fearing it is enchanted to enslave her soul as human legends suggest.

A second High Fae named Lucien enters the room and expresses his outrage over the death of his friend, Andras. Lucien mocks Feyre’s appearance and scoffs at the idea that such a scrawny mortal could have killed a powerful sentinel. The master of the house, whom the servants call Tamlin, defends Feyre and commands Lucien to behave after he insults her. Tamlin reveals that he already knows Feyre’s name and orders a servant named Alis to take her to a room for a bath. Alis, who wears a bird mask, leads Feyre away while Lucien continues to argue that Feyre is a burden who should be killed.

Feyre is bathed, groomed, and plucked by masked servants in a bedroom larger than her entire family cottage. She refuses to wear a turquoise velvet dress and insists on having her old clothes returned, which Alis reveals have fallen apart. Alis eventually provides her with a fine tunic and trousers, noting that Feyre is dangerously thin after the long winter. Alis warns Feyre to keep her ears open and her mouth shut, as many in the house resent her for Andras’s death. Feyre learns that Lucien is an emissary and that Tamlin’s mercy is the only thing currently ensuring her safety.



### Chapter 7
Feyre returns to the dining room where Tamlin and Lucien are drinking from solid gold goblets. Tamlin repeatedly assures her that the food is safe to eat and points her toward an empty chair at the end of the table. Lucien mocks Tamlin for his rusty social skills with females, noting that he hasn't interacted with a mortal in decades. Tamlin serves Feyre a heaping plate of meat and wine himself, claiming it is an honor for a human to be served by High Fae. Feyre eats the meal in silence while the two males watch her closely and offer awkward, stilted compliments about her cleanliness.

Feyre asks if she is to be a slave, but Tamlin clarifies that he does not keep slaves and she can do whatever she wants with her time. She begs to be released so she can care for her starving family, but Tamlin insists she must stay to fulfill the Treaty. Lucien demands to know the details of Andras’s death, and Feyre describes shooting him with an ash arrow and skinning his pelt. Tamlin reveals that he has already used magic to ensure Feyre's family is well-cared for and provided with a sustainable source of income. He swears on his life and possessions that they are safe, which Feyre finds difficult to believe given her distrust of faeries.

Tamlin uses invisible magical bonds to force Feyre to sit and finish her meal after she tries to kick back her chair in anger. He warns her that if she attempts to cross the wall or flee, he will stop providing for her family. After she finishes eating, the food magically vanishes from the table to prevent her from overindulging and making herself sick. Lucien questions why she finds their company so unpleasant and asks if she has a lover or suitors back in her village. Feyre admits she was close to a man named Isaac but denies being in love with him before retiring to her room for the night.



### Chapter 8
Feyre wakes early in her new lavish bedroom and reflects on how her sisters might be reacting to her absence. She rigs a trip-wire rope from the curtains to snap in the face of anyone entering her room as a warning system. Alis enters and triggers the trap, laughing at the human's futile attempt to defend herself against immortal beings. After a breakfast of bacon and tea, Feyre explores the silent halls and is captivated by a lifelike still-life painting of flowers in a glass vase. She realizes the High Fae are far more civilized and artistic than the feral monsters described in human folklore and legends.

In the garden, Feyre encounters Tamlin dressed in warrior’s clothes and refuses his offer for a guided tour of the estate. Tamlin explains that a magical blight has been infecting Prythian for nearly fifty years, causing their magic to act strangely and diminish. He reveals that the masks they wear are the permanent result of a magical surge that occurred during a masquerade long ago. Andras had been across the wall specifically to search for a cure to this sickness, which threatens to eventually spread to the mortal realm. Tamlin admits he has no personal interest in destroying the wall or attacking humans, unlike some other members of his kind.

Feyre spends the day mapping the garden paths and searching for hidden locations where she might stash weapons or supplies. She feels the presence of invisible, giggling creatures in the garden but Alis interrupts before she can identify what they are. During dinner, Feyre successfully steals a knife from the table and hides it in her sleeve while Lucien and Tamlin are distracted. Lucien teases her about her age and hunting skills, leading Feyre to realize that she is only nineteen and has spent most of her life in survival mode. Tamlin offers his condolences for the death of Feyre's mother, though Feyre remains cold and dismissive of his sympathy.



### Chapter 9
Alis informs Feyre that Lucien is on border patrol, prompting Feyre to head toward the stables to catch him alone. Tamlin intercepts her in the garden and offers a ride across the grounds, but Feyre bluntly rejects his company to pursue her own plans. At the stables, she finds Lucien mounted on a black horse and he invites her to join him on a hunt in the southern woods. Feyre is provided with a white mare and a heavy yew bow, which is much larger than the weapon she used at home. As they ride, she notes the forest is unnervingly empty of other faeries or High Fae sentinels.

Lucien accurately guesses that Feyre joined him to persuade him to find a loophole in the Treaty that would allow her to go home. He informs her that there is no alternative to the Treaty’s demands and she is stuck at the estate for the rest of her life. Lucien explains that the masks they wear are an honor to Tamlin’s shape-shifting gifts, though they now wish they could remove them. He mentions a mysterious and frightening female figure who might be responsible for the blight, but he refuses to provide a name. Feyre learns that Tamlin can shift his sentries into animal forms, which is how Andras appeared as a wolf in the mortal woods.

Feyre asks about other magical beings, and Lucien mentions the Suriel, ancient and wicked creatures that will answer any question if trapped. Lucien suddenly becomes terrified and orders Feyre to stare straight ahead and not react to anything she sees or feels. A creeping, leeching cold surrounds them, and Feyre hears an ancient voice in her head threatening to grind her bones and feast on her marrow. This entity, called the Bogge, becomes real and lethal only if an observer acknowledges its presence by looking at it directly. Feyre successfully resists the urge to look and focuses on pleasant memories until the invisible monster finally departs into the brush.



### Chapter 10
Lucien explains that the Bogge is a creature that should not be in their lands and represents the worsening state of the magical blight. He admits he is a warrior but confesses that he is not as skilled in combat as Tamlin. Feyre snaps at Lucien for being a prick, which surprisingly makes him grin and eases the tension between them. When they return for dinner, Tamlin is visibly irritated that Feyre spent the day with Lucien after claiming she wanted to be alone. Lucien informs Tamlin that they encountered the Bogge in the forest, causing Tamlin to immediately crush his metal fork in a fit of rage.

Tamlin demands to know the exact location of the sighting and prepares to hunt the creature down himself. He stalks out of the dining room with lethal intent, leaving Feyre and Lucien to finish the meal in silence. Lucien explains that while the Bogge cannot be killed by normal means, Tamlin possesses the unique power required to face and destroy it. Feyre is struck by the contrast between the gruff man who served her dinner and the warrior capable of hunting immortal horrors. She returns to her room and sharpens her stolen knife on a stone while watching the garden for Tamlin’s return.

As the moon rises, Feyre continues her vigil at the window, curious to see if Tamlin survived his encounter with the Bogge. She eventually spots a hunched figure lurking near the hedges and moving toward the light of the house. Feyre initially assumes it is the High Lord returning from his hunt, but she realizes the physical proportions are wrong. The figure hobbles closer into the light, revealing a face she recognizes instantly from her life across the wall. Feyre experiences a shock of recognition as she identifies the man standing in the garden as her own father.
